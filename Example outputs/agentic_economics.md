## 1. Background & Motivation  

The rapid diffusion of generative AI, large‑language models (LLMs) and autonomous software agents has created a new class of economic actors that can **decide, transact and reinvest without direct human supervision**.  These “agents” may be purely digital (LLM assistants, smart contracts), physical (autonomous robots, drones) or hybrid (edge‑enabled IoT devices).  Traditional economic theory, which treats firms and households as the only decision‑making units, struggles to capture the **production, financing and risk‑profile** of such autonomous factors of production.  

The emerging field of **Agentic Economics** therefore seeks to:  

* Define agents and the **four axioms** that make them economically meaningful – Agency, Autonomy, Accountability and Alignment (A1‑A4) [Web‑1].  
* Provide a **valuation framework** (Agentic Free Cash Flow, A‑FCF) and accounting standards (A‑GAAP) for agents [Web‑1].  
* Explore how agents can be **capitalized** through Agentic Value Tokens (AVT) and integrated into broader financial markets [Web‑1].  
* Assess the **systemic implications** for markets, regulation, and macro‑economic stability as billions of agents begin to operate at scale [Web‑2], [Web‑3], [Web‑5].  

Motivation stems from three converging trends:  

1. **Technological readiness** – LLMs now support multi‑step planning, tool use and persistent memory, enabling agents that can execute end‑to‑end business processes [Web‑5].  
2. **Economic incentive** – Agents can monetize API calls, data licensing, and transaction fees, generating measurable cash flows that investors can price [Web‑1].  
3. **Policy pressure** – Regulators are drafting “Know‑Your‑Agent” (KYA) and AI‑specific liability rules, making a standardized economic description essential for compliance [Web‑4].

| **Macroeconomic Modelling** | What are the aggregate effects of **agent‑driven capital flows** (AVT trading) on monetary policy transmission and financial stability? | Agentic capital could bypass traditional intermediaries, altering the effectiveness of interest‑rate tools. |
| **Regulatory Architecture** | How should “Know‑Your‑Agent” (KYA) standards be operationalised across jurisdictions, and how will liability be allocated for autonomous decisions? | Divergent AI‑Act regimes risk regulatory arbitrage and systemic risk amplification [Web‑4]. |
| **Risk Metrics** | Which extensions of traditional risk measures (Sharpe, beta) best capture **inter‑agent correlation risk** and **model‑amortisation volatility**? | Agentic portfolios exhibit novel risk drivers (e.g., model‑drift, data‑license volatility). |
| **Tokenomics & Governance** | What optimal **AVT governance structures** balance token holder incentives with the need for rapid technical upgrades (model amortisation) without centralised control? | Over‑centralisation defeats the autonomy axiom (A2) while token fragmentation can erode liquidity. |
| **Ethics & Alignment** | How can **ANI mechanisms** be calibrated to incorporate societal externalities (environmental, inequality) in a way that is transparent to both regulators and token holders? | Mis‑aligned incentives could exacerbate “AI‑driven” inequality or ecological footprints. |
| **Cross‑Domain Agent Interaction** | How will agents from disparate sectors (e.g., health, logistics, finance) negotiate protocols and resolve conflicts when their objectives diverge? | Multi‑agent protocols (A2A, AP2) need robust arbitration and dispute‑resolution layers. |

| **Human–Agent Collaboration** | What design patterns maximize **human oversight** while preserving agent autonomy, especially in high‑stakes domains (medical, autonomous transport)? | Prevents catastrophic failures and addresses public trust concerns. |
| **Scalability of Audits** | Can zero‑knowledge proof systems scale to audit millions of simultaneous agent ledgers without prohibitive compute cost? | Auditable transparency is the cornerstone of the Accountability axiom (A3). |
